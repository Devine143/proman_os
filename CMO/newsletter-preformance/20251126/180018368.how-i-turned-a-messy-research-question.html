The Framework That Changed Everything

I came across something called the Pyramid Principle, created by Barbara Minto at McKinsey.

At first I brushed it off—“communication framework.”
I needed research help.

I was wrong.

The core idea:

Start with the answer. Then support it with grouped arguments. Then back those with evidence.

It flips the school model (build up to a conclusion) upside-down.

But here’s the real insight:

The Pyramid Principle isn’t just a communication tool. It’s a thinking tool.

It shapes how you research, how you reason, and how you structure conclusions.

Combine that with AI, and everything changes.

SCQA: The Four Letters That Structure Any Research Question

The Pyramid Principle includes a sub-framework called SCQA:

S — Situation: What’s the agreed context?
C — Complication: What changed? What’s the tension?
Q — Question: What specifically needs answering?
A — Answer: Your hypothesis (you refine it later).

Here’s my aluminium import SCQA:

Situation: We’re specifying windows/doors for a 9-storey mixed-use building in Cape Town. Tight budget. 10–12 month window for delivery.

Complication: Chinese imports may be cheaper—but SANS compliance, quality risks, and 60–75 day shipping create major schedule threats.

Question: Should we import or buy local, and under what conditions does each option make sense?

Answer (hypothesis): Local seems safer due to schedule sensitivity. Import only makes sense as a hybrid approach with strong risk controls.

I didn’t know this upfront.
But hypothesizing forced my research to become directional instead of aimless.

You shift from “collect information” to “test a hypothesis.”

MECE: How to Organize Research So Nothing Falls Through the Cracks

The other tool: MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive).

It forces you to create categories that don’t overlap and cover the full problem.

My MECE buckets:

  1. Regulatory Compliance

  2. Financial Analysis

  3. Supplier Due Diligence

  4. Logistics & Lead Time

  5. Risk & Mitigation

  6. Decision Framework

Each bucket is distinct. Together they cover everything required for a real decision.

And here’s the breakthrough:

AI becomes 10x more effective when you give it discrete buckets instead of broad questions.

Instead of:

“Tell me about importing aluminium.”

You give it:

“Research regulatory requirements for aluminium windows in South Africa. Focus on SANS 613, SANS 204, and SANS 10400-N. Produce a compliance checklist and required documentation.”

You stop boiling the ocean. You fill buckets.

The Transformation in Practice

1. Executive Summary — Answer First

Top of the report:

Key Finding:
Chinese imports offer 15–30% savings on FOB pricing, but hidden costs (port fees, clearing, insurance, duties) add 15%+ to CIF pricing. When schedule risk is included, imports may cost more than local procurement.

Recommendation:

  • Schedule-critical projects: Local

  • Hybrid (import standard items only): Possible

  • Full import: Not advised without strong risk controls

2. Landed Cost Analysis — Substack-Friendly Version

Chinese Import Costs

  • Product (FOB): R1,300–2,700/m²

  • Shipping & port fees: ±R50k per container

  • Duties: ±15% (HS Code 7610.10)

  • Hidden costs: ±15% of CIF

Local Costs

  • Product: ±R3,500/m²

  • Minimal hidden/indirect costs

  • Faster lead time, fewer unknowns

Insight:
The apparent 20–30% saving shrinks to 15–25%, and a single shipping or compliance delay can wipe out remaining savings entirely.

3. Decision Framework — Substack-Friendly Diagram

Decision Flow

  • If delay tolerance is zero → Local

  • If compliance is complex → Local for critical elements

  • If strict QC/inspection is not possible → Local

  • If schedule buffer < 3 months → Local

  • Otherwise → Hybrid import may be explored for standard units

This is what a decision-ready document looks like—not “information,” but clarity.

A Critical Note on Verification

AI output is not fact.

For this project, I manually verified:

  • SANS requirements

  • SARS tariff schedules

  • Freight rates

The Pyramid Principle didn’t remove the need for verification—it made it manageable.

You only fact-check the high-impact numbers, because the framework shows you where they are.
